Do Migratory Wildebeest Improve Soil Carbon Stocks in the Mara-Serengeti?

Using 128,000+ GPS sightings, satellite-derived NDVI, and a 25,000 km² spatial database built in PostGIS, I mapped how wildebeest grazing shapes grassland vegetation and soil organic carbon across one of Africa's most iconic ecosystems.

The Question

Every year, over a million wildebeest thunder across the Mara-Serengeti in one of Earth's greatest wildlife spectacles. But beyond the spectacle, what do they do to the land? Specifically — does their grazing enhance or deplete soil organic carbon (SOC), a critical store of terrestrial carbon and an indicator of ecosystem health?

This question matters. Grasslands cover 40% of Earth's land surface and store about 34% of terrestrial carbon. Understanding how large herbivores influence that carbon store has direct implications for climate modelling, conservation policy, and land management.

"Wildebeest exhibit forage-tracking behaviour, concentrating grazing pressure in areas of high vegetation productivity — and that grazing appears to stimulate further growth."

Seasonal Migration & Vegetation Response

Wildebeest don't graze randomly — they track rainfall. Using GPS telemetry data spanning 5 seasons alongside MODIS-derived NDVI, I found a strong correlation between precipitation, vegetation greenness, and herd distribution.

Seasonal Rainfall (mm) vs Mean NDVI
Peak NDVI follows peak rainfall by 4–6 weeks. Wildebeest concentrate in Jan–Mar (wet season) when vegetation productivity is highest.

The relationship is clear: Jan–Mar wet season drives the highest NDVI (0.493), while Aug–Oct dry season produces the lowest (0.251). Wildebeest track this signal precisely, concentrating grazing in high-productivity areas.

Does Grazing Increase Soil Carbon?

The headline finding: after controlling for elevation (the primary driver of SOC via its effect on temperature and moisture), grazed areas within the Protected Area show significantly higher residual SOC than ungrazed areas.

Mean SOC by Area Type (t C/ha)
Grazed areas within the Protected Area (51.3 t C/ha) show comparable raw SOC to ungrazed areas (52.4 t C/ha), but residual SOC analysis reveals a different story after accounting for terrain.
Residual SOC — Grazed vs Ungrazed (t C/ha)
After removing elevation's effect via OLS regression, grazed areas show a mean residual of +2.16 t C/ha — indicating MORE carbon than terrain alone predicts. Ungrazed areas average −3.14 t C/ha.

"Grazed areas hold 2.16 t C/ha more carbon than their elevation would predict — while ungrazed areas hold 3.14 t C/ha less. A difference of 5.3 t C/ha attributable to grazing history."

What Drives SOC? A Regression Story

OLS regression across all 5,000 sample points identified three significant predictors of SOC:

Standardised Effect Size on SOC (|β|)
Elevation dominates (β=0.67), followed by grazing history (β=0.32). Rainfall contributes marginally (β=0.05). R²=0.379.
1
Elevation is the primary SOC driver (β=0.67) Higher elevations have lower temperatures → slower decomposition → more carbon accumulation. This is why we normalise for elevation to isolate the grazing effect.
2
Grazing has a significant positive effect (β=0.32) Wildebeest dung, urine, and hoof action stimulate microbial activity and return nutrients to soil. The grazing optimization hypothesis: moderate grazing increases net primary productivity and root exudation.
3
Rainfall matters less than expected (β=0.05) Within the semi-arid Mara-Serengeti, rainfall variation at 0.05° grid resolution is insufficient to override the dominant elevation and grazing effects on SOC.

Protected Area vs Buffer Zone

A spatial comparison between the Protected Area (where wildebeest concentrate) and a 20km buffer zone (lower grazing pressure) reveals stark differences in vegetation response:

Mean NDVI — Protected Area vs 20km Buffer Zone
The Protected Area shows a 7× larger seasonal NDVI gain (Δ+0.066) compared to the buffer zone (Δ+0.015), suggesting that concentrated grazing amplifies vegetation response to rainfall.

Methods Summary

Spatial database: PostGIS with 10 raster tables covering SOC (IDW from 5,000 field samples), 30m DEM, seasonal rainfall (2019), and NDVI by grazing class. Study area: ~25,000 km² across Tanzania and Kenya.

Wildebeest GPS data: 128,704 georeferenced sightings from 2013–2018, filtered and binned seasonally (Jan–Mar, Apr–May, Jun–Jul, Aug–Oct, Nov–Dec).

SOC residuals: OLS regression of SOC on elevation across all grid cells. Residuals = actual SOC − predicted SOC from terrain. Positive residuals indicate carbon enrichment beyond what terrain alone explains.

Statistical tests: Independent samples t-test (grazed vs ungrazed SOC, p<0.001), Pearson correlation (NDVI vs rainfall r=0.89), Kruskal-Wallis (NDVI across grazing intensity classes).
